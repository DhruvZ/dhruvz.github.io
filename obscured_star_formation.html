Tracing the History of Obscured Star Formation
with the SIMBA Cosmological Galaxy Evolution Simulation

My first grad school project involved studying the amount of dust-obscured star formation across the history of the Universe. Previously observational results (e.g. Whitaker 2017) had seen that for >85% of the history of the Universe, the amount of ultraviolet light tracing star formation that had been reprocessed by dust into the infrared was relatively constant across time. They also found that the biggest galaxies were the ones where the highest fraction of UV light was absorbed.

I attempted to recreate their results using the high-resolution SIMBA simulation 25 Mpc/h box, which includes a dust model, and running galaxies from it across time through radiative transfer to make mock spectra. We actually recovered their results and found that the relationship between galaxy mass and blocked UV light held up relatively well up to z~3. We explained that more massive galaxies having higher fractions of UV light absorbed is a reflection of the fact that massive galaxies have reached more dust per stellar mass than smaller galaxies. We also predicted that earlier than z~3, the relationship between mass and obscured light fraction would change and that dust would actually have more of an impact! We tied this to the evolving relatively distributions of stars and dust within galaxies. Recently, Casey et al. published an updated observational analysis of dust in galaxies and found results that matched our predictions!
