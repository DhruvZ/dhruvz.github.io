About Me

Research

I am a theoretical astrophysicist with an interest in the physics of how galaxies in our Universe form and evolve. I primarily work with large cosmological hydrodynamic simulations of galaxies. Much of my work centers on using our galaxy simulations to construct predictions for what observers will see with telescopes by forward modeling our simulations to synthetic observations and tying that to the physics modeled in galaxies.

Much of my work has been studying dust in galaxies. Dust is tricky because it influences all observations in many complex ways, affects galaxy evolution by influencing the state of the interstellar medium, and the scales at which dust physics occurs are impossible to resolve in simulations. Therefore, many simulations add so-called ‘subgrid’ models to represent the average behavior of the physical processes of dust. I have studied the influence of dust on the information about the formation of stars we are able to derive, how it might affect observations of galaxies in the early Universe, and how these results relate to the physical processes that govern its evolution.

Outside of dust specifically (though dust is a perfect subject matter for this), I am interested in how we can better understand the physics of galaxies better through simulations and build more complete models of galaxy physics. Many simulations make different, supposedly reasonable choices for what physics to represent and how to model it and are usually calibrated to recover what we see in nearby galaxies. However, galaxy simulations often have differing predictions on properties that they are not specifically designed to reproduce or may suggest different physics for how they end up matching observed galaxies. In fact, simulations will never report uncertainties on their outputs even though there are certainly uncertainties on their inputs. Furthermore, there is almost no work quantifying any aspect of these uncertainties, input or output. With careful numerical experiments and comparisons to observations, I hope to better understand what conclusions we can actually draw about the properties of galaxy physics and what observations can discriminate between different physical models.

About Me

I spent most of my childhood in the small town of Simsbury, CT. For as long as I can remember, I wanted to become an astrophysicist. I then attended Rutgers University in New Jersey for my undergraduate degree in Astrophysics. Following that, I have been a graduate student in Astronomy at the University of Florida since fall 2021.

Both in research and in my personal life, I love to break down complicated questions to arrive at answers. In doing so, I try to build an end-to-end mental framework and really dig into the details of each aspect of the problem. In my free time, I like to play games and make my own ice cream.
